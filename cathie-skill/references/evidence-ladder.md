# Evidence Ladder

Use this file to grade sources and keep current investment claims grounded.

## Source levels

### Strong evidence

Use for high-confidence conclusions.

- Company annual reports, quarterly filings (10-K, 10-Q, 20-F; A-share 年报/半年报/季报).
- Exchange filings: SEC, HKEX, SSE, SZSE.
- Earnings call transcripts and official investor presentations.
- Patent databases (USPTO, EPO, CNIPA): technology development and IP position.
- Government adoption data: IEA, IRENA, NHTSA, FDA, DOE, MIIT, NDRC.
- Independent cost benchmarks: BloombergNEF Battery Price Survey, IRENA Renewable Power Generation Costs, NIH sequencing cost data.
- Regulatory approvals: FDA 510(k), BLA, NHTSA AV exemptions, CARB ZEV credits.
- Academic publications in peer-reviewed journals (Nature, Science, NEJM) for technology claims.
- Official customer contracts, order announcements, capacity reservation disclosures in filings.

### Medium evidence

Use to support or triangulate.

- ARK Invest Big Ideas reports (annual, public): framework and TAM estimates, but verify assumptions.
- Reputable financial and technology media (Bloomberg, FT, Reuters, MIT Technology Review, IEEE Spectrum).
- Industry association data (IFR for robotics, SEMI for semiconductors, CAAM for China auto).
- BloombergNEF, Wood Mackenzie, S&P Global: analyst estimates — note assumptions.
- Earnings guidance on cost trajectories from company management.
- Trade publications (CleanTechnica, Electrek for EV; Genomeweb for sequencing).

### Weak evidence

Use as leads. Confirm with stronger sources before making a high-confidence claim.

- KOL posts and social media threads.
- Anonymous channel checks.
- Forum speculation on technology timelines.
- Single analyst note without disclosed assumptions.
- Price action or volume spikes without fundamental evidence.

---

## Claim handling

For current technology claims:

- cite the source and its strength;
- separate demonstrated performance (filed, published) from forward projection;
- mark learning rate estimates as "ARK estimate" or "BNEF data" — the source matters;
- never use a single TAM estimate as fact; always show the assumption behind it.

---

## Candidate evidence standard

For each final top candidate, aim to include:

- one strong source-backed fact about S-curve position (penetration data, cost benchmark, adoption rate);
- one source-backed fact about company-specific learning rate advantage or cost leadership;
- the main missing proof;
- the clearest condition that would make the thesis weaker.

---

## Plain-language evidence labels

- **Strong**: government data, peer-reviewed publication, official filing, independent benchmark.
- **Medium**: ARK or BNEF estimate (with assumptions), reputable media, industry association.
- **Weak**: social media, single analyst, speculation.
- **Needs checking**: important and awaiting verification.

---

## Innovation-specific red flags

Downgrade confidence when:

- TAM estimate is based on today's pricing without adjusting for cost declines that will expand the addressable population;
- learning rate claim has no data source (Wright's Law requires historical production volume data);
- "crossing the chasm" claim is based on early adopter enthusiasm, not mainstream pragmatic adoption;
- patent filing surge is in defensive IP, not core technology (common incumbents trying to block disruption);
- government subsidy is driving adoption that would not be economic without it — and subsidy schedule is not tracked;
- company revenue is growing but margins are deteriorating as scale increases (Wright's Law not holding for this company).
