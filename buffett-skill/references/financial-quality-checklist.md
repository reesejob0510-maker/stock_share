# Financial Quality Checklist

Use this file when assessing whether a business has the financial profile of a high-quality compounder.

## Why financial quality matters

Buffett's framework rewards patience, so the financial quality check is designed to answer: can this business sustain above-average returns for 10+ years without requiring external capital? A beautiful moat with poor financial execution is still a poor investment.

---

## 1. Profitability

### Return on Equity (ROE)

- **Target**: > 15% consistently over 5 years.
- **Red flag**: ROE below 12% or highly variable year-to-year.
- **Adjustment**: Run Du Pont decomposition to separate profitability, efficiency, and leverage effects.
  - ROE = Net margin × Asset turnover × Equity multiplier
  - High ROE from high leverage (equity multiplier > 3×) is a warning sign, not a quality signal.

### Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)

- **Target**: Materially above WACC (Buffett informally uses 10% as his hurdle).
- ROIC = NOPAT / (Equity + Net Debt)
- A business earning 20% ROIC while its cost of capital is 8% is creating real value.
- **Red flag**: ROIC trending down despite revenue growth (possible moat erosion).

### Gross Margin

- **What to look for**: Stable or expanding gross margin over 5+ years.
- Compare against direct competitors and industry average.
- A gross margin 10+ pp above the industry average is a strong moat signal.
- **Red flag**: Gross margin compressing despite volume growth — possible pricing pressure.

### Operating Leverage

- Does margin expand as revenue grows?
- Positive operating leverage is a sign of scale economics.
- **Red flag**: Margin stays flat or declines even in revenue growth periods.

## 2. Cash Flow Quality

### Owner Earnings (Buffett's preferred measure)

> *"Owner earnings represent (a) reported earnings plus (b) depreciation, depletion, amortization, and certain other non-cash charges... less (c) the average annual amount of capitalized expenditures for plant and equipment that the business requires to fully maintain its long-term competitive position."*
> — Warren Buffett, 1986 Berkshire letter

**Formula**: Owner Earnings = Net income + D&A − Maintenance Capex

**How to estimate maintenance capex**: Use the lower of capex guidance and D&A, adjusted for known expansion spending. When in doubt, use historical average capex minus known growth projects.

### Free Cash Flow (FCF)

- FCF = Operating cash flow − Total capex
- FCF / Net income ratio: should be close to 1.0 over time. Sustained ratio below 0.7 warrants scrutiny.
- FCF yield (FCF / Market cap) as a valuation cross-check.

### Cash Flow Consistency

- FCF should be positive in most years; cyclical dips acceptable, chronic deficits are not.
- Check 7–10 year history when data is available.

### Working Capital Discipline

- Days Sales Outstanding (DSO): stable or declining = pricing power.
- Inventory Days: rising inventory without revenue growth = demand issue.
- Cash Conversion Cycle: shorter is better; improvement over time signals operational quality.

---

## 3. Balance Sheet

### Leverage

- **Buffett's preference**: low debt. "We want companies that would survive a prolonged period of tight credit."
- Net Debt / EBITDA: caution above 2×; concern above 3×.
- Net Debt / Owner Earnings: how many years of earnings to pay off debt?

### Interest Coverage

- EBIT / Interest expense: > 5× is healthy; < 3× needs attention.

### Off-Balance-Sheet Obligations

- Operating leases (now mostly on balance sheet post-IFRS 16 / ASC 842).
- Pension underfunding: check the notes; can be significant for older industrials.
- Contingent liabilities: regulatory fines, litigation, warranties.

### Cash Position

- Is the company sitting on excess cash? Is management deploying it intelligently?
- Excessive cash on the balance sheet for years without deployment = capital allocation concern.

---

## 4. Capital Allocation

This is where Buffett spends most time beyond the moat analysis.

### The capital allocation hierarchy

1. Reinvest in the business at high ROIC (organic growth, R&D, capacity expansion where returns are clear).
2. Bolt-on acquisitions at reasonable prices in adjacent areas.
3. Return capital: dividends and buybacks when the stock trades below intrinsic value.
4. Large acquisitions as a last resort — and only at sensible prices.

### Evaluating management's track record

- **Acquisitions**: What were the multiples paid? Did they create or destroy value? Check goodwill growth vs ROIC on acquired assets.
- **Buybacks**: Were they done at prices below intrinsic value, or mechanically regardless of valuation? Share count trend vs stock performance.
- **Dividends**: Consistent and growing is a good sign; erratic or funded by debt is not.
- **Retained earnings**: Has each dollar retained over 5 years generated more than $1 of market value?

### Owner-mindset signals

- Insider ownership significant and growing.
- CEO compensation aligned to long-term metrics, not revenue or short-term EPS.
- Management speaks about the business in owner terms (cash generation, per-share value, ROIC) rather than revenue and EBITDA.
- Berkshire-style plain-language shareholder communication (honest about failures).

## 5. Red Flags

| Signal | What it may mean |
|---|---|
| Revenue grows, FCF declines | Accounting quality concern; aggressive capitalization of costs |
| Inventories + receivables growing > 2× revenue growth | Demand may be weaker than reported; collection risk |
| Goodwill as large % of assets with declining ROIC | Value-destroying acquisitions |
| Serial equity issuance | Management diluting shareholders; business may need capital |
| Gap between adjusted EPS and GAAP EPS widens each year | Non-recurring items becoming recurring; aggressive adjustments |
| Gross margin stable but net margin shrinking | Overhead cost discipline failing |
| Capex guidance consistently below actual spending | Maintenance capex understatement; owner earnings overstated |
| Related-party transactions (A-share specific) | Corporate governance risk; cash leakage |
| Large insider selling into price strength | Insider view on overvaluation or business deterioration |

## Summary scorecard (quick check)

| Metric | Target | Source |
|---|---|---|
| ROE (5-year avg) | > 15% | Annual reports |
| ROIC | > 10% (above WACC) | Annual reports + estimate |
| Gross margin trend | Stable / improving | Income statement |
| Owner earnings FCF conversion | ≥ 0.8× | Cash flow statement + capex footnotes |
| Net debt / EBITDA | < 2× | Balance sheet |
| Interest coverage | > 5× | Income statement |
| Buyback timing | Below intrinsic value | 10-K / proxy + stock history |
| Insider ownership | Meaningful stake | Proxy / exchange filing |
