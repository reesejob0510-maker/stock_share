# Evidence Ladder

Use this file to grade sources and keep current investment claims grounded.

## Source levels

### Strong evidence

Use for high-confidence conclusions.

- Annual reports (10-K, 20-F, A-share 年报/半年报), quarterly filings (10-Q, 季报).
- SEC, HKEX, SSE, SZSE, and other exchange filings.
- Earnings call transcripts and official investor presentations.
- Shareholder letters (Berkshire Hathaway annual letters are primary source material for methodology).
- Official customer contracts, order announcements, capacity reservation disclosures.
- Regulatory filings, project approvals, patent filings, standards documents.
- Proxy statements (DEF 14A): compensation, insider ownership, governance structure.

### Medium evidence

Use to support or triangulate.

- Reputable financial media (WSJ, FT, Bloomberg, Reuters, Caixin, 财联社).
- Trade publications and industry association data.
- Sell-side research when assumptions are visible and the analyst is named.
- Company website, product pages, IR materials not in formal filings.
- Competitor filings used for cross-checking (e.g., customer concentration data).

### Weak evidence

Use as leads. Confirm with stronger sources before making a high-confidence claim.

- KOL posts and social media threads.
- Anonymous forum discussions.
- Unverified channel checks.
- Screenshots with unclear origin.
- Price action or volume spikes without fundamental evidence.

---

## Claim handling

For current security-specific claims:

- cite or name the source type;
- separate confirmed facts from interpretation;
- mark weak claims as leads;
- avoid building a top-ranked candidate on weak evidence alone.

---

## Candidate evidence standard

For each final top candidate, aim to include:

- one strong source-backed fact about moat quality (pricing power, switching cost evidence, network density);
- one source-backed fact about financial quality (ROE trend, FCF conversion, capital allocation history);
- the main missing proof;
- the clearest condition that would make the thesis weaker.

---

## Plain-language evidence labels

Use these labels in final answers:

- **Strong**: supported by filings, official announcements, transcripts, regulatory documents, or hard technical documents.
- **Medium**: supported by credible media, trade publications, specialist analysis, or cross-company public evidence.
- **Weak**: based on social discussion, rumor, unexplained price action, or early leads.
- **Needs checking**: important and awaiting verification with available tools.

---

## Red flags for financial evidence

Downgrade confidence when:

- the moat thesis depends on a single customer relationship without filing confirmation;
- gross margin improvement is driven by a one-time input cost decline, not structural pricing power;
- ROE improvement is driven by leverage increase rather than margin or efficiency improvement;
- FCF consistently trails net income by more than 20% without clear explanation;
- goodwill grows faster than earnings in an acquisition-heavy business;
- related-party transactions are material and disclosed in footnotes only (A-share specific);
- management guidance for capital allocation has been repeatedly changed.
