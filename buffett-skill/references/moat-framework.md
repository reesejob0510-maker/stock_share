# Moat Framework

Use this file when analyzing whether a business has a durable competitive advantage.

## The five moat sources

A moat is a structural advantage that allows a business to earn returns above its cost of capital for an extended period. Buffett's public letters and interviews describe five recurring sources.

### 1. Brand and pricing power

**What it looks like**
- Customers pay a premium for the brand name even when comparable alternatives exist.
- The company raises prices regularly without losing meaningful volume.
- Brand preference persists across economic cycles and demographic shifts.

**Evidence signals**
- Gross margin consistently 10+ percentage points above industry peers.
- Price increases in recent earnings calls without volume pushback.
- Consumer survey data or market share data showing brand loyalty.
- The product is "habitual" — purchased on autopilot, not compared each time.

**Examples from Berkshire letters**: Coca-Cola, See's Candies, GEICO (brand trust), Apple (ecosystem + brand).

**Downgrade signals**
- Private-label alternatives gaining meaningful share.
- Brand sentiment declining in customer data.
- The company relying on promotion/discount to maintain volume.
- A younger demographic not adopting the brand at the same rate.

---

### 2. Network effects

**What it looks like**
- Each additional user makes the product more valuable to existing users.
- The network becomes harder to dislodge as it grows, not easier.
- Competitors cannot easily pay their way in; the value is intrinsic to the scale.

**Evidence signals**
- User growth accompanied by rising engagement metrics (not just accounts).
- Customer acquisition cost declining as the network scales.
- Evidence that customers prefer the platform because of who else is on it.
- Data showing multi-homing is difficult or sticky.

**Examples**: Payment networks (American Express, Visa), financial data platforms, marketplace businesses.

**Downgrade signals**
- Multi-homing becomes cheap and easy.
- A competing network achieves critical mass in a key geography or segment.
- Platform regulation fragmenting the network.

---

### 3. Switching costs

**What it looks like**
- Customers face meaningful time, money, or operational pain if they switch to a competitor.
- The switching cost is embedded in the product design, not just inertia.
- Revenue per customer grows over time even without volume growth (land-and-expand).

**Evidence signals**
- Net revenue retention above 100% (SaaS/services businesses).
- Long contract durations or multi-year renewal patterns.
- Customers have integrated the product into core workflows.
- Churn rates low even when competitors offer lower prices.

**Downgrade signals**
- A competitor offering migration tools or subsidized switching.
- Open standards emerging that reduce lock-in.
- Customer consolidation reducing per-customer value.

---

### 4. Cost advantages

**What it looks like**
- The company produces or delivers at materially lower cost than competitors.
- The cost advantage comes from scale, geography, proprietary process, or unique inputs.
- The advantage is structural, not cyclical.

**Evidence signals**
- Operating cost per unit declining as volume grows.
- Gross margin above industry average sustained over multiple cycles.
- Evidence of process innovation or proprietary supply chain.
- Competitors with similar scale unable to match the cost structure.

**Examples**: GEICO (direct-to-consumer model vs agent-based), Burlington Northern (rail density), BYD (battery manufacturing scale).

**Downgrade signals**
- A new entrant with a fundamentally cheaper production method.
- Input cost advantage eroding due to supply changes.
- Regulatory or labor cost increases disproportionately hitting the business.

---

### 5. Regulatory and license moats

**What it looks like**
- A government license, patent portfolio, or regulatory approval creates a legal barrier to competition.
- The barrier is not easily circumvented; new entrants need the same approval or face structural disadvantage.
- The moat is often paired with one of the above economic moats.

**Evidence signals**
- Licensed business operating in a jurisdiction with limited competitor licenses.
- Patent expiry schedule showing long runway of protection.
- Regulatory approval process that takes years and significant capital.
- History of regulators blocking or delaying new entrants.

**Downgrade signals**
- Patent cliff approaching (common in pharma).
- Regulatory environment shifting to open access or forced sharing.
- Technology enabling circumvention of the regulated activity.

## Moat width rating

After analyzing moat sources, assign a rating:

| Rating | Description |
|---|---|
| **Wide** | At least one strong moat source with 10+ year durability evidence; ROE > 15% consistently |
| **Narrow** | Real advantage but limited duration or scale; some erosion signals present |
| **None** | No identifiable structural advantage; earnings driven by cycle or pricing environment |
| **Uncertain** | Moat story is plausible but lacks strong filing-level evidence |

---

## Moat durability checklist

Before assigning "Wide", confirm:

- [ ] Moat source is structural, not cyclical.
- [ ] Gross margin stable or improving over 5 years.
- [ ] ROE consistently above 15% without excessive leverage.
- [ ] Pricing power demonstrated in recent periods (price increases without volume loss).
- [ ] No credible technology or business model disruption in the next 5–7 years.
- [ ] Management behavior consistent with protecting the moat (not over-expanding).

---

## Common moat traps

**"Great product" ≠ moat**: A product customers love is not a moat unless it generates pricing power, switching costs, or network density. Popularity can reverse.

**"Market leader" ≠ moat**: Market share leadership is a result, not a source of advantage, unless it comes with scale economics or network density.

**"Technology advantage" ≠ moat**: Technology changes quickly. A tech lead is a moat only if it is embedded in customer workflows (switching costs) or platforms (network effects).

**High ROE from leverage ≠ moat**: Always run the Du Pont decomposition. ROE = (Net margin) × (Asset turnover) × (Leverage). High ROE from leverage is a risk, not a moat.
